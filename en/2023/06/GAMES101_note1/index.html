Barycentric Coordinates

As explained in the GAMES101 course, barycentric coordinates—especially those for triangles—are extremely useful in computer graphics. They make it convenient to interpolate information at a triangle’s vertices across the triangle’s surface.

When I first listened to the lecture, I still had many questions. For example, why must the three coefficients sum to 1 and all be nonnegative for a point to lie inside the triangle?

After thinking about it for some time, I felt that understanding the concept according to the literal meaning of its name was intuitive: understand it from the perspective of a center of mass.

Physical Perspective

Suppose there is a triangle whose three vertices have masses Ma,Mb,McM_a, M_b, M_cMa​,Mb​,Mc​, respectively, while every region other than the vertices has mass 0. According to the definition of a center of mass, the triangle’s center of mass is:

center of mass=MaA+MbB+McCMa+Mb+Mc\text{center of mass} = \frac{M_aA + M_bB + M_cC}{M_a + M_b + M_c} center of mass=Ma​+Mb​+Mc​Ma​A+Mb​B+Mc​C​

Separating these three terms gives:

AMaMa+Mb+Mc+BMbMa+Mb+Mc+CMcMa+Mb+McA\frac{M_a}{M_a + M_b + M_c} + B\frac{M_b}{M_a + M_b + M_c} + C\frac{M_c}{M_a + M_b + M_c} AMa​+Mb​+Mc​Ma​​+BMa​+Mb​+Mc​Mb​​+CMa​+Mb​+Mc​Mc​​

This form is extraordinarily similar to the barycentric-coordinate expression αA+βB+γC\alpha A + \beta B + \gamma CαA+βB+γC: every term is a coefficient multiplied by a vertex coordinate.

Looking at a term of the form MaMa+Mb+Mc\frac{M_a}{M_a + M_b + M_c}Ma​+Mb​+Mc​Ma​​, we find that the coefficients used to calculate the center of mass satisfy the barycentric-coordinate requirements exactly: α+β+γ=1\alpha + \beta + \gamma = 1α+β+γ=1, and no term is negative.

Thinking in terms of the physical center of mass, what exactly are we converting when we transform ordinary coordinates into barycentric coordinates?

Map the coefficients in the center-of-mass formula to those in barycentric coordinates, namely MaMa+Mb+Mc→α\frac{M_a}{M_a + M_b + M_c} \to \alphaMa​+Mb​+Mc​Ma​​→α. We can then see that these coefficients are actually the proportions of the triangle’s total mass assigned to each vertex.

In other words, during the conversion—suppose we convert point ppp from Cartesian coordinates to barycentric coordinates—we are really solving this problem: how should weight be distributed among the triangle’s three vertices so that the triangle’s center of mass lies at point ppp?

The converted coordinate (α,β,γ)(\alpha, \beta, \gamma)(α,β,γ) is precisely the proportion of weight assigned to each of the triangle’s three vertices.

This makes it easy to understand why the three numbers must sum to 1 and be nonnegative for a point to lie inside the triangle. First, from a physical perspective, if mass is nonnegative, the center of mass must lie inside the object. Second, to satisfy the definition of a center of mass, α\alphaα, β\betaβ, and γ\gammaγ must sum to 1. Since they represent the proportions of the total mass at the three vertices, the three numbers necessarily sum to 1, corresponding to the total mass.

Of course, barycentric coordinates are a generalization of the physical definition, so a purely algebraic explanation may be more convincing.

Algebraic Perspective

We know that a triangle’s barycentric coordinates use a linear combination of its three vertices to represent a coordinate: p=αA+βB+γCp = \alpha A + \beta B + \gamma Cp=αA+βB+γC. We can now try to explain algebraically why the three coefficients must sum to 1 and be nonnegative for the point to lie inside the triangle.

First, we can express point ppp in another form that will help with the later derivation:

p=A+uAB→+vAC→p = A + u\overrightarrow{AB} + v\overrightarrow{AC} p=A+uAB+vAC

Note that uuu and vvv have no relation to the three barycentric-coordinate coefficients here. This expression means that, starting from point A, we move uuu units along the direction AB and then vvv units along the direction AC to reach point ppp.

First, one thing is certain if the point is to remain inside the triangle: uuu and vvv are nonnegative. Starting at A, moving any distance only in the direction BA→\overrightarrow{BA}BA or CA→\overrightarrow{CA}CA would immediately leave the triangle.

Rearranging the preceding expression slightly gives:

p−A=uAB→+vAC→pA→=uAB→+vAC→uAB→+vAC→−pA→=0p - A = u\overrightarrow{AB} + v\overrightarrow{AC} \\ \overrightarrow{pA} = u\overrightarrow{AB} + v\overrightarrow{AC} \\ u\overrightarrow{AB} + v\overrightarrow{AC} - \overrightarrow{pA} = {0} p−A=uAB+vACpA​=uAB+vACuAB+vAC−pA​=0

This expression can be understood as follows: we move every point, including point ppp, by −OA→-\overrightarrow{OA}−OA. At this point, A must lie at the origin.

The expression then becomes intuitive. First, the vector uAB→+vAC→u\overrightarrow{AB} + v\overrightarrow{AC}uAB+vAC takes us from the origin to point ppp; then pA→\overrightarrow{pA}pA​ takes us from point ppp back to A, which is the origin.

Because these two parts point in opposite directions and have the same magnitude—one travels from A to ppp, and the other from ppp to A—the sum of the two vectors must be 0.

Expanding and rearranging the expression gives:

u(B−A)+v(C−A)+A−p=0uB−uA+vC−vA+A−p=0A(1−u−v)+uB+vC−p=0(1−u−v)A+uB+vC=p\begin{align*} u(B - A) + v(C - A) + A - p &= 0 \\ uB - uA + vC - vA + A - p &= 0\\ A(1 - u - v) + uB + vC - p &= 0 \\ (1 - u - v)A + uB + vC &= p \end{align*} u(B−A)+v(C−A)+A−puB−uA+vC−vA+A−pA(1−u−v)+uB+vC−p(1−u−v)A+uB+vC​=0=0=0=p​

This form is exactly the same as the barycentric-coordinate expression, so we can determine that α=1−u−v\alpha = 1 - u - vα=1−u−v, β=u\beta = uβ=u, and γ=v\gamma = vγ=v.

Adding these quantities gives 1−u−v+u+v=11 - u - v + u + v = 11−u−v+u+v=1. Indeed, the three barycentric-coordinate coefficients necessarily sum to 1.

We already established that uuu and vvv are nonnegative, but if u+v>1u + v > 1u+v>1, would that not make α\alphaα negative?

The length of the projection of uAB→u\overrightarrow{AB}uAB onto AM→\overrightarrow{AM}AM is therefore uAB→⋅AM→∣AM→∣u\overrightarrow{AB} \cdot \overrightarrow{AM} \over |\overrightarrow{AM}|∣AM∣uAB⋅AM​.

Because projection, or equivalently the dot product, is a linear operation, if uuu is 0.5, the projection’s length must be half of ∣AM→∣|\overrightarrow{AM}|∣AM∣.

We can therefore say that the length of the projection of AB→\overrightarrow{AB}AB onto AM→\overrightarrow{AM}AM is u∣AM→∣u|\overrightarrow{AM}|u∣AM∣.

For point ppp to remain inside the triangle, the length of the projection of the vector uAB→+vAC→u\overrightarrow{AB} + v\overrightarrow{AC}uAB+vAC onto AM→\overrightarrow{AM}AM must be no greater than the length of AM→\overrightarrow{AM}AM itself. The projected length of AB→\overrightarrow{AB}AB onto AM→\overrightarrow{AM}AM is u∣AM→∣u|\overrightarrow{AM}|u∣AM∣, while the projected length of AC→\overrightarrow{AC}AC onto AM→\overrightarrow{AM}AM is v∣AM→∣v|\overrightarrow{AM}|v∣AM∣. Naturally, the projected length of the sum of the two vectors onto AM→\overrightarrow{AM}AM is (u+v)∣AM→∣(u + v)|\overrightarrow{AM}|(u+v)∣AM∣.

As stated above, for point ppp to stay inside the triangle, the projection of uAB→+vAC→u\overrightarrow{AB} + v\overrightarrow{AC}uAB+vAC onto AM→\overrightarrow{AM}AM must be no longer than AM→\overrightarrow{AM}AM itself. That is, (u+v)∣AM→∣≤∣AM→∣(u + v)|\overrightarrow{AM}| \le |\overrightarrow{AM}|(u+v)∣AM∣≤∣AM∣, so u+v≤1u + v \le 1u+v≤1.

Because α=1−u−v\alpha = 1 - u - vα=1−u−v and u+v≤1u + v \le 1u+v≤1, we can show that α\alphaα must also be nonnegative.

At this point, we have explained why a point inside a triangle must satisfy α+β+γ=1\alpha + \beta +\gamma = 1α+β+γ=1, with every coefficient nonnegative. Of course, converting a Cartesian coordinate into barycentric coordinates still requires some relatively complicated calculations. For this part, I think the first method described in this blog post is relatively easy to understand and quite ingenious; interested readers can take a look.
