The Euclidean algorithm states that gcd⁡(a,b)=gcd⁡(b,r)\gcd(a,b)=\gcd(b,r)gcd(a,b)=gcd(b,r).

Let ddd be any common divisor of aaa and bbb, and let:

a=dm, b=dnr=a−bq=dm−(dn)q=d(m−nq)a = dm,\ b = dn\\ \begin{align*} r &= a - bq\\ &= dm - (dn)q\\ &= d(m - nq) \end{align*} a=dm, b=dnr​=a−bq=dm−(dn)q=d(m−nq)​

Since r=d(m−nq)r=d(m-nq)r=d(m−nq), if aaa and bbb have any common divisor ddd, then ddd is also a common divisor of rrr and bbb (except when r=0r=0r=0; in that case the greatest common divisor is aaa). ax+by=gcd⁡(a,b).ax+by=\gcd(a,b). ax+by=gcd(a,b).

Modular Multiplicative Inverse

The modular multiplicative inverse of a mod pa\bmod pamodp is the solution xxx of ax≡1(modb)ax\equiv1\pmod bax≡1(modb).

A modular inverse is somewhat like an additive inverse under a modulus.

exgcd

When aaa and bbb are coprime, exgcd can solve this problem. Since gcd⁡(a,b)=1\gcd(a,b)=1gcd(a,b)=1, extended Euclid solves ax+by=1ax+by=1ax+by=1.

Rewrite ax≡1(modb)ax\equiv1\pmod bax≡1(modb) as:

ax≡1(modb)ax mod b=1ax−bk=1.ax\equiv1\pmod b\\ ax\bmod b=1\\ ax-bk=1. ax≡1(modb)axmodb=1ax−bk=1.

Setting y=−ky=-ky=−k gives ax+by=1ax+by=1ax+by=1.

One of xxx and yyy in ax+by=1ax+by=1ax+by=1 may be negative. A negative yyy causes no problem, but if xxx is negative, the answer we obtain is not the smallest positive integer among all feasible values of xxx.

Looking at ax+by=1ax+by=1ax+by=1, we can add a multiple of bbb to xxx, transforming the expression into a(x+bn)+b(y+an)=1a(x+bn)+b(y+an)=1a(x+bn)+b(y+an)=1 (note that bbb is negative, so the abnabnabn terms cancel). This lets us make xxx positive without changing the equality ax+by=1ax+by=1ax+by=1.

We can therefore write:

Assume first that xxx is negative.

The first x % b adds some multiples of bbb to xxx, turning it into the largest negative number that still satisfies the condition. For example, suppose bbb is 13 and xxx is -25. After x = x % b, xxx becomes -12, which is equivalent to adding 13 to xxx.

The following +b turns this largest valid negative number into the smallest valid positive number. For example, x+b=−12+13=1x+b=-12+13=1x+b=−12+13=1. What, then, is the purpose of the final % b?

It handles the case where xxx was positive to begin with. By subtracting suitable multiples of bbb from xxx, it reduces xxx to the smallest positive number satisfying the condition.

Linear Recurrence

The linear recurrence computes the modular inverses of all integers from 111 to nnn modulo a prime ppp in O(n)O(n)O(n) time. ppp must be prime to ensure all values in the range are coprime to it.

Because this is a recurrence algorithm, it needs an initial condition. It is easy to see that the inverse of 1 modulo any integer is 1 itself, because 1×1=11\times1=11×1=1. This gives us the initial condition.

Suppose the recurrence has now reached the number iii. Write p÷i=k…rp\div i=k\ldots rp÷i=k…r, so p=ki+rp=ki+rp=ki+r. Converting this to a congruence gives:

ki+r≡0(modp).ki+r\equiv0\pmod p. ki+r≡0(modp).

Let i−1i^{-1}i−1 and r−1r^{-1}r−1 denote the modular inverses of iii and rrr modulo ppp, respectively. Multiplying both sides of the congruence by i−1r−1i^{-1}r^{-1}i−1r−1 and expanding gives:

kr−1+i−1≡0(modp),kr^{-1}+i^{-1}\equiv0\pmod p, kr−1+i−1≡0(modp),

so:

i−1≡−kr−1(modp).i^{-1}\equiv-k r^{-1}\pmod p. i−1≡−kr−1(modp).

The simplification uses i−1i≡1(modp)i^{-1}i\equiv1\pmod pi−1i≡1(modp), with the same relationship holding for rrr and r−1r^{-1}r−1. Since k=⌊p/i⌋k=\lfloor p/i\rfloork=⌊p/i⌋ and r=p mod ir=p\bmod ir=pmodi:
