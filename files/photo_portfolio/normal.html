Invasive Species

Works in this series earned an honorable mention in Scholastic Art & Writing, and this series (the complete version) earned an official selection in the International Photography Award.

Captured in Nanhu Farm, an abandoned collective farm in Hangzhou, China, this series explores the interplay between human ambition and nature’s resilience. The warehouses, consumed by creeping vegetation piercing shattered windows, crumbled ceilings, and fractured walls, symbolize humanity as an invasive species. Once epitomizing industrial progress, feeding burgeoning cities, they now crumble under nature’s quiet reclamation.

Capturing nature’s wonders, from Iceland’s aurora to Arizona’s Petrified Forest, deepened my appreciation of it. Though I was passionate about computers for a long time, at the Cornell Model UN, I learned how AI’s energy demands worsen global warming, sparking reflection on the unintended consequences of human advancement. This tension is reflected in the project, which invites reflection on humanity’s impact and nature’s resilience. s

Iridescent Cellophane World

This series (first three images) earned an official selection in the International Photography Award.

The photo was taken at the first few weeks of processing my own films. Armed with only black-and-white film and chemicals, I wondered how I could capture the rainbow’s colors outside my window.

As a STEM student, I sometimes apply technological methods to my image making. Inspired by how screens display colors – mixing red, green, and blue light sources in different proportions, I took three photos in the same position at different time, using optical filters to restrict the wavelengths of light. I then merged the color layers in post-production.

While researching the history of photography, I discovered my method mirrored the trichrome technique, one of the earliest ways to record color photos. This realization filled me with awe, as if I’d unknowingly retraced the steps of photographic pioneers.

This photo creates a dream-like sense, transforming everyday objects in my room into something extraordinary. The surreal overlap of colors reminds viewers that even the mundane can hold wonder and adventure.

Gaze

This series (the complete version) earned an official selection in the International Photography Award.

During a one-day assignment at the Staten Island Ferry as part of the photojournalism course at The School of the New York Times, I noticed that everyone was gazing. Each passenger was lost in their own world—some fixated on their phones, others staring at the water or the distant cityscape.

With this series, I try to capture these private, unseen moments of introspection and observation. What were they thinking about? Perhaps their trip to New York, their work, their dreams, or just the fleeting beauty of the harbor. By documenting these glances, I invite the viewer to contemplate the spiritual world of these individuals or themselves.

This project was also a reflection on the human condition—the shared yet solitary nature of our experiences.

 

 

 

 

Swinger

Captured in Central Park during The School of New York Times photojournalism course, this series uses motion blur to emphasize the rhythm and energy of a person swinging amidst a blurred backdrop. The dynamic scene reflects the fleeting joy of leisure, even in a city as fast-paced as New York.

 

Hong Kong

Works in this series earned honorable mention and silver key from Scholastic Art & Writing and an official selection from the International Photography Award.

The 2019-2020 Hong Kong protests were a series of demonstrations against the introduction of a bill to amend the Fugitive Offenders Ordinance in regard to extradition [wikipedia]. The protest was initiated concerning the legislative independence of Hong Kong, which, according to some, might not be retained if criminals are allowed to be extradited to Mainland China.

Hong Kong holds a special place in my heart. My younger brother was born there, and frequent family trips made the city feel like a second home. When the protests erupted, I felt a deep urge to document these historic moments.

I made these images intuitively, drawn to the unfolding stories in front of me, in the context of my enduring interest in documenting history as it emerges.

 

Industrial Tranquility

Works in this series earned a Scholastic Art & Writing national silver medal, regional silver key, and an International Photography Award official selection.

Taken at a harbor in Pinghu, China, this photo blends industry with serenity as human development is viewed from the air. I grew up listening to my mother’s stories about Pinghu, so I wanted to revisit the place and see it through my camera lens with fresh eyes.

Unlike the close-up chaos of everyday life of our post-industrialization world of various social issues, the view from above filters out the noise and clutter, revealing a sense of order in the industrial world. Ships, docks, and the sea form a harmonious composition of geometric shapes and muted colors.

This perspective reminds me that taking a step back, both physically and emotionally, can change the way we view the seemingly mundane. For me, this is more than just a photograph - it reflects how distance can reveal balance and serenity in the most unexpected places.

Iceland

Amid Iceland’s quiet white, a pastel-roofed church nestles beneath an aqua sky, its calm mirrored in drifting clouds and serene negative space. Nearby, the aurora spills its ghostly green over a star-brushed night, illuminating a lone house on the snowfield below.

The two photos earned an honorable mention and gold key from Scholastic Art & Writing, respectively.

Incompatible

This work earned an honorable mention from Scholastic Art & Writing.

Taken at a political rally in Cincinnati, Ohio, the photo juxtaposes adults and the child in the same frame, strategically comparing their differences. The adults, wearing identical red hats and focusing their view in the same direction, convey a sense of uniformity and anonymity. Meanwhile, the young girl in the foreground, spinning in a carefree dance, radiates individuality and joy, which conveys naivety and uniqueness through her bold and unrestrained dance. She is totally in her own world. The composition strengthens this dichotomy by including a clear boundary between the adults and the child.
